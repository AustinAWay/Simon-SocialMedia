Ever wonder why there's a gas station on every corner, but only one big mall for the whole city? There's actually a hidden rule behind it.

It's called Central Place Theory, and it's all over your AP Human Geography exam.

The idea: low-order services you use every day — like coffee or gas — need to be close, so there are tons of them.

But high-order services, like a stadium or a specialty hospital, have a huge range. People will travel far for them, so a region only needs a few.

So the map of any city isn't random. It's a nested pattern of big and small centers — and now you'll never unsee it.
